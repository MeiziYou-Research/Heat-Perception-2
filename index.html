Abstract

Urban heat is increasingly recognised as a core challenge for urban liveability and climate justice, yet we know little about how residents emotionally interpret it in everyday life. Here we propose an attention–emotion framework that conceptualises urban heat perception as couplings between which heat-related issues attract public attention and which emotions they elicit, applied to warm-season tweets from 50 large cities.

Using a large language model as a closed-set classifier, we label tweets with attention categories and discrete emotions to construct city-comparable indicators. We find a shared backbone of recurrent mechanisms across cities: everyday burdens related to sleep, work and mobility are typically framed in sadness, health alerts are strongly associated with fear, and systems and governance discourse is mostly neutral with pockets of anxiety.

At the same time, we identify context-sensitive mechanisms in which similar thermal conditions are narrated either as abnormal shocks or as normal background. These differences are closely linked to 15-minute accessibility and environmental inequalities. Our framework moves beyond aggregate sentiment towards a mechanism-based, multi-channel architecture of urban heat perception, offering guidance for more context-sensitive climate adaptive policy.

Project

This website visualises how selected cities express, attend to, and explain urban heat stress through a multi-layer attention–emotion framework.

It combines global comparison, city-level exploration, and mechanism-based profiling to support more context-sensitive understanding of climate adaptation and urban inequality.
